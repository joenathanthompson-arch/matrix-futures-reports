## Instrument Commentary

**GC and SI** remain mildly constructive rather than aggressively bullish. The current setup still benefits from the weaker-dollar backdrop and from supply-driven inflation pressure, but the model is restrained by a neutral Fed, flat real yields, and stale but still negative ETF-flow information for gold.[5] [13]

**CL** remains slightly bullish. The positive case rests on the persistence of geopolitical and supply-side disruption, while the main offset is the latest official U.S. inventory build.[7] [8] This keeps crude constructive, but not yet in a high-conviction breakout category.

**ES and NQ** remain neutral. Nasdaq still receives support from a stronger semiconductor backdrop, but soft growth and the broader macro mix are not sufficiently favorable to push either large-cap U.S. index into a higher-conviction bullish tier.[5] [14]

**YM and RTY** remain the weakest U.S. equity contracts in the framework. Their construction is more sensitive to cyclical growth, credit conditions, and the yield curve than ES or NQ, and all three of those inputs remain unhelpful.[5] [10] [11]

**M6E** remains slightly bullish. The combination of a soft dollar and a still-firm ECB communication backdrop is enough to keep the euro constructive, even though the euro-area growth outlook remains less convincing than the AUD story.[2] [3]

**6A** remains the standout bullish signal. The key supports are a **hawkish RBA**, **China PMI back above 50**, and **firm copper**, all of which continue to reinforce AUD's cyclical and policy-sensitive upside case.[4] [9] Because this is a catalyst-sensitive score, it should be treated as a high-opportunity but faster-decay signal.

**6J** remains slightly bullish. The yen continues to benefit from the softer-dollar regime and from a Bank of Japan backdrop that is no longer deeply out of step with global normalization, even though the latest official BoJ meeting itself was a hold.[2] [6]

The cleanest expression remains to **favor AUD strength over broad U.S. cyclical equity exposure**. That means **6A** remains the highest-conviction long bias in the report, while **YM** and **RTY** remain the least attractive relative exposures.
